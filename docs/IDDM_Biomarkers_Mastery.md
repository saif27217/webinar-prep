# IDDM (Type 1 Diabetes) Biomarkers — Complete Webinar Prep Guide

---

## 1. FUNDAMENTALS — What is IDDM?

**IDDM = Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus = Type 1 Diabetes (T1D)**

- Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic β-cells → absolute insulin deficiency
- Accounts for 5–10% of all diabetes
- Two subtypes: **classical** (immune-mediated) and **idiopathic** (no detectable autoimmunity)
- Hallmark: β-cell destruction leads to loss of C-peptide production and dependence on exogenous insulin

**Pathogenesis in brief:**
Genetic susceptibility (HLA) + Environmental trigger → Loss of immune tolerance → T-cell mediated β-cell destruction → Autoantibody production (serological markers) → Progressive β-cell loss → Clinical diabetes

## 2. THE FIVE ISLET AUTOANTIBODIES (The Core Biomarkers)

### 2.1 Anti-GAD65 (Anti-Glutamic Acid Decarboxylase 65) — GADA

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Target** | GAD65 enzyme (converts glutamate → GABA in β-cells) |
| **Prevalence at onset** | 70–80% of new-onset T1D |
| **Persistence** | Long-lived; persists years after diagnosis |
| **Best for** | Adult-onset autoimmune diabetes (LADA screening) |
| **Sensitivity** | ~53% in adult-onset autoimmune diabetes |
| **Specificity** | ~93% |
| **Clinical note** | First-line test in adults; positive LR+ ≈7.34 |
| **Association** | Associated with faster β-cell decline when present early |

**Key teaching point:** GADA is the MOST SENSITIVE single marker for adult-onset T1D and autoimmune diabetes in general. Think of it as the "adult marker."

### 2.2 Anti-IA-2 (Anti-Insulinoma-Associated Antigen-2) — IA-2A

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Target** | Intracellular tyrosine phosphatase (protein tyrosine phosphatase-like antigen) |
| **Prevalence at onset** | 55–75% |
| **Persistence** | Declines over time after diagnosis |
| **Best for** | Confirming autoimmune T1D; combined with GADA improves sensitivity |
| **Specificity** | Very high (highest among autoantibodies) |
| **Clinical note** | More common in children; predicts progression in relatives |

**Key teaching point:** IA-2A is the most SPECIFIC autoantibody. When present, it's a very strong confirmation of autoimmune T1D.

### 2.3 Anti-IAA (Insulin Autoantibodies)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Target** | Insulin itself (the first autoantigen targeted) |
| **Prevalence at onset** | 50–70% in children; less common in adults |
| **Best for** | Very early prediction in children; appears BEFORE clinical onset |
| **Persistence** | Can be affected by exogenous insulin therapy |
| **Clinical note** | Most commonly the FIRST autoantibody to appear in young children |
| **Assay challenge** | RIA is gold standard; non-radioactive assays still have variable inter-lab performance |

**Key teaching point:** IAA is often the FIRST antibody to appear in genetically susceptible children and is the PREDICTOR of early onset. The assay is technically difficult — RIA remains gold standard but non-radioactive formats are improving.

### 2.4 Anti-ZnT8 (Anti-Zinc Transporter 8) — ZnT8A

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Target** | Zinc transporter 8 (SLC30A8) — important for insulin crystallization |
| **Prevalence at onset** | 60–80% (especially in children) |
| **Discovery** | 2007 (newest of the autoantibodies) |
| **Best for** | Adds sensitivity when GADA/IA-2A are negative; independent prognostic value |
| **Clinical note** | FDA Class II cleared ZnT8 ELISA exists (BioVendor) |
| **Prognostic** | ZnT8A positivity in single-autoantibody relatives significantly increases 4-year progression risk |

**Key teaching point:** ZnT8A "fills the gap" — when other antibodies are negative, ZnT8A catches an additional 10–15% of cases. Panel sensitivity goes from ~80% → >90% when ZnT8A is added.

### 2.5 Anti-IA-2β (Anti-Phogrin)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Target** | IA-2β (related to IA-2 but distinct protein) |
| **Prevalence** | Less commonly tested; overlaps with IA-2A |
| **Clinical use** | Research mainly; adds marginal diagnostic value over IA-2A |

## 3. HLA TYPING & GENETIC RISK

### 3.1 HLA Class II (the major genetic determinant)

**~50% of T1D genetic risk comes from HLA region (chromosome 6p21)**

| Haplotype | Risk Level | Frequency in T1D | Frequency in Controls |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **DR3-DQ2** | HIGH | ~40–50% | ~25% |
| **DR4-DQ8** | HIGH | ~40–50% | ~30% |
| **DR3/DR4 heterozygote** | HIGHEST | ~35–40% | ~3–5% |
| **DR2-DQ6** | PROTECTIVE | ~10% | ~30% |

**Key teaching points:**
- **DR3/DR4 heterozygotes** have the highest risk (OR up to 15–20 for some studies)
- BUT even with DR3/DR4, absolute progression to T1D is <10% without additional markers
- HLA typing is reimbursed under molecular pathology CPT codes
- Used in screening programs and trial enrollment

### 3.2 Non-HLA Genetic Variants

- **INS VNTR** (insulin gene VNTR) — 2nd strongest genetic risk factor after HLA
- **CTLA-4** — immune checkpoint gene
- **PTPN22** — lymphoid tyrosine phosphatase (R620W variant)
- **IL2RA** — interleukin-2 receptor α-chain
- **Over 60 non-HLA loci** identified via GWAS

### 3.3 Polygenic Risk Scores (PRS/GRS)

- Combine HLA + non-HLA variants into single score
- AUC 0.82–0.90 for T1D vs other diabetes
- Can identify newborns with >>background risk by age 6
- Higher GRS → earlier onset, lower C-peptide at diagnosis
- **Combined Risk Score (CRS)** merges genetics + family history + autoantibody count → AUC ≥0.90

## 4. C-PEPTIDE — The Functional Biomarker

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **What it is** | 31-amino acid peptide cleaved from proinsulin during insulin secretion |
| **Source** | Equimolar with insulin from β-cells |
| **Why measure it** | Endogenous insulin production (not affected by exogenous insulin injections) |
| **Test of choice** | Mixed-Meal Tolerance Test (MMTT) — stimulates maximum β-cell response |
| **Alternative** | Fasting C-peptide (easier but less sensitive) |
| **Prognostic** | Higher C-peptide at diagnosis → better early glycemic control |
| **Monitoring** | Declining C-peptide = ongoing β-cell destruction |
| **Threshold** | <0.6 nmol/L postprandial = severe insulin deficiency (ADA criteria) |

**Key teaching point:** C-peptide is your ONLY direct measure of how much functional β-cell mass remains. It's what separates "how bad is it now?" from "is it autoimmune?"

**Limitations:**
- Not standardized across labs
- Assay variability
- C-peptide has a long half-life (~30 min) but can be degraded by kidneys in CKD patients

## 5. STAGING OF TYPE 1 DIABETES (ADA/Endocrine Society 2024)

| Stage | Autoantibodies | Dysglycemia | Symptoms | β-cell Function |
|-------|---------------|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Stage 1** | ≥2 autoantibodies | Normal | None | Normal or declining |
| **Stage 2** | ≥2 autoantibodies | Abnormal (impaired fasting/glucose tolerance) | None | Declining |
| **Stage 3** | ≥2 autoantibodies | Overt hyperglycemia | Clinical symptoms (polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss) | Significantly reduced |

**Why staging matters:**
- Stage 1–2 can be detected YEARS before clinical diabetes
- Screening at-risk relatives identifies pre-symptomatic individuals
- **Teplizumab** (anti-CD3) FDA-approved for Stage 2 → delays progression to Stage 3 by ~2 years
- Enables prevention trials and early intervention

## 6. ASSAY PLATFORMS — How We Measure Biomarkers

| Platform | Pros | Cons | Use |
|----------|------|------|-----|
| **RIA** | Gold standard for IAA; very high specificity | Radioisotopes, licensing, low throughput | Legacy; some IAA testing |
| **ELISA** | High throughput, quantitative, widely available | Proportional bias vs RIA; not interchangeable for longitudinal monitoring | Most clinical labs |
| **CLIA** | Automated, high throughput | Same bias issues as ELISA | Large screening programs |
| **ECL (MSD platform)** | High specificity (near 100%); low CVs (single-digit); multiplex capable | Lower sensitivity than RIA for some antibodies | Research + clinical |
| **Simoa (Single Molecule Array)** | Ultra-high sensitivity (5–8× for GAD65); sub-picomolar detection | Expensive; not yet widely clinical | Emerging; future of early detection |

**Key teaching point:** The trend is toward multiplex + high-sensitivity platforms. ECL allows simultaneous detection of multiple autoantibodies. Simoa enables detection of VERY low titers that may predict disease years before onset.

**Standardization issue:** The IASP/IASS workshops show wide performance variability across labs, especially for IAA. This is a major limitation — results from different labs may not be directly comparable.

## 7. EMERGING & ADVANCED BIOMARKERS

### 7.1 Post-Translationally Modified Autoantigens
- **Citrullinated** and **deamidated** GAD65/other autoantigens
- These modifications create **neo-epitopes** that escape immune tolerance
- Citrullination (by PAD enzymes) and deamidation (by transglutaminase) **enhance binding to diabetes-susceptible HLA alleles**, especially DRB1*04:01
- Drive autoreactive T and B cell responses NOT seen against native proteins
- McGinty et al. (2014): demonstrated T-cell recognition of PTM-GAD65 epitopes in T1D subjects
- **Still in research phase** but may explain why standard autoantibody panels miss some patients

### 7.2 Proinsulin:C-Peptide Ratio — New Predictive Biomarker
- **Sims et al. (2023, Diabetologia)**: High proinsulin:C-peptide ratio identifies Stage 2 T1D patients at HIGH risk for progression
- This ratio also **predicts response to teplizumab** — those with high ratio respond better
- Mechanism: reflects β-cell stress/ER stress — cells producing abnormal insulin processing
- **Cited 27 times** — emerging as a critical biomarker in prevention trials

### 7.3 Machine Learning + Multi-Omic Biomarkers
- **Mittal et al. (2025, IJMS)**: Systematic review of ML for T1D early detection
- Combines autoantibodies, HLA, CGM data, metabolomics, transcriptomics
- AI-driven risk scoring achieves better discrimination than single biomarker panels

### 7.4 Endotyping & Personalized Medicine
- **Pipella & Thompson (2026, Diabetes Obes Metab)**: Toward personalized medicine in T1D
- Patient heterogeneity (age, disease stage, endotype, biomarkers) influences therapeutic efficacy
- Teplizumab may work better in certain endotypes — biomarker-guided treatment selection

### 7.2 T-cell Assays
- Measuring autoreactive T-cells directly
- More directly reflects the pathogenic process
- Technically challenging; not yet routine

### 7.3 MicroRNA & Metabolomic Profiles
- Blood-based signatures that may predict progression
- Research phase

### 7.4 Neonatal Screening Programs
- Combined PRS + autoantibody panels
- Finnish/DALDA studies pioneered this approach
- Identifies at-risk children before any autoimmunity develops

## 10. FREQUENTLY ASKED WEBINAR QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

**Q: Why is ZnT8A important if we already have GADA and IA-2A?**
A: ZnT8A catches an additional 10–15% of patients that the traditional two-antibody panel misses. It's especially valuable when GADA and IA-2A are negative but clinical suspicion remains high. It also has independent prognostic value — single-antibody-positive relatives who are ZnT8A-positive have significantly higher progression risk.

**Q: Can we use C-peptide to diagnose T1D?**
A: C-peptide confirms insulin deficiency but doesn't differentiate autoimmune from non-autoimmune causes. It's a FUNCTIONAL marker, not a diagnostic marker. You need autoantibodies for diagnosis and C-peptide for monitoring disease severity/progression.

**Q: Why is IAA testing so problematic?**
A: The gold-standard RIA for IAA requires radioisotope handling and licensing. Non-radioactive alternatives (ELISA, CLIA) show significant variability between labs and aren't directly comparable. The IASP/IASS standardization workshops demonstrate this persistent problem.

**Q: What's the role of HLA typing in adults?**
A: Less useful than in children because ~95% of Caucasians carry at least one risk HLA allele. HLA typing is most valuable in: (1) newborn screening, (2) distinguishing T1D from T2D in ambiguous cases, (3) identifying high-risk relatives, (4) trial enrollment.

**Q: How does teplizumab fit into the biomarker framework?**
A: Teplizumab (anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody) is FDA-approved for Stage 2 T1D (≥2 autoantibodies + dysglycemia without symptoms). It delays progression to clinical diabetes by ~2 years. The staging that defines eligibility relies entirely on autoantibody + C-peptide biomarkers.

**Q: What's the future direction?**
A: (1) Neonatal screening with combined genetic + autoantibody panels, (2) ultra-sensitive assays (Simoa) for detecting very early disease, (3) post-translationally modified autoantigens as new predictive markers, (4) multi-omic profiles (metabolomics + transcriptomics) for risk stratification.

**Q: What is the proinsulin:C-peptide ratio and why does it matter?**
A: This ratio reflects β-cell ER stress. When β-cells are stressed, they produce incomplete proinsulin that isn't fully processed to insulin+C-peptide. A HIGH ratio means more proinsulin relative to C-peptide = stressed β-cells = faster decline. Sims et al. (2023) showed it predicts both progression to Stage 3 AND response to teplizumab. This is an emerging biomarker in prevention trials.

**Q: How do post-translational modifications relate to T1D?**
A: Enzymes like PAD (citrullination) and transglutaminase (deamidation) modify β-cell proteins, creating neo-epitopes that the immune system hasn't been tolerized to. These modified proteins bind more strongly to HLA-DRB1*04:01 and trigger stronger T-cell responses. This may explain why some patients develop T1D despite "negative" standard autoantibody panels.

**Q: What is endotyping in T1D?**
A: Just as breast cancer has molecular subtypes, T1D may have different "endotypes" based on the dominant immune pathway (T-cell vs B-cell), metabolic profile, and rate of β-cell decline. Personalized medicine approaches are beginning to match patients to therapies based on their endotype rather than treating all T1D the same.

**Q: How does machine learning change biomarker analysis?**
A: ML algorithms can integrate multiple data types — autoantibodies, HLA, CGM patterns, metabolomics — into composite risk scores that outperform any single biomarker. This is particularly useful for predicting WHO will progress and WHEN, which is critical for prevention trial design.

**Q: Is IA-2 or GAD65 more clinically useful?**
A: IA-2 Ab is more predictive for future T1D development, but GAD65 Ab assay is more sensitive. They complement each other — you need both in the panel. (Source: Harrison 22nd, p.84)

**Q: Can GAD65 be elevated in non-diabetic autoimmune conditions?**
A: Yes. GAD65 is elevated in 93% of Stiff-man syndrome, and also in myasthenia gravis, Lambert-Eaton syndrome, and acquired cerebellar ataxia. It's also a marker for coexisting autoimmune diseases (Graves', Hashimoto's, Addison's, pernicious anemia). Always consider the clinical context.

**Q: What is LADA and why does it matter for biomarkers?**
A: 5-10% of white adults with a T2D phenotype actually have islet cell autoantibodies (especially GAD65) — this is Latent Autoimmune Diabetes of Adults. They're more likely to require insulin within 5 years. GAD65 testing is the key discriminator. Misdiagnosis as T2D is common and leads to delayed insulin therapy.

**Q: Can immunotherapy cause T1D?**
A: Yes — immune checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4) can trigger rapid-onset T1D. HLA-DR4 is present in ~70% of these cases. This is an increasingly recognized complication as cancer immunotherapy expands.

## 12. ISPAD 2024 CLINICAL PRACTICE GUIDELINES

The International Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Diabetes published updated consensus guidelines (Haller et al., Horm Res Paediatr 2025):

**Key Recommendations:**
1. **Universal screening**: Autoantibody screening for all first-degree relatives of T1D patients
2. **Staging system**: Adopted 3-stage classification (Stage 1/2/3)
3. **Teplizumab**: Recommended for Stage 2 T1D to delay progression
4. **C-peptide monitoring**: At diagnosis and longitudinally to track β-cell function
5. **HLA typing**: For risk stratification in newborn screening programs
6. **Preservation trials**: Enrollment should use multimarker panels
